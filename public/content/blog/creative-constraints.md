Every project I've built has taught me: **limitation enables creativity**.

## The Trap of Infinite Choice

Give someone Ableton Live with 10,000 samples and unlimited tracks. Watch them freeze.

Give someone a 303, an 808, and four bars. Watch them make acid house.

## Constraints I Intentionally Introduce

### 1. **Palette Limitation**
TMC-CL1 deliberately ships with only 16 drum samples. Not because we couldn't include more—because *analysis paralysis* is the enemy of flow state.

Users can swap samples, but the interface forces: **work with what's in front of you first**.

### 2. **Time Limits**
When prototyping FlueBricks designs, I enforce: **one iteration per day**. Print overnight, test in the morning, redesign by evening.

This sounds restrictive. It's actually freeing. You can't second-guess yourself when the printer is already running.

### 3. **Format Constraints**
Blog posts: 500-1000 words max. Forces clarity. If I can't explain it in 1000 words, I don't understand it yet.

Project demos: 60 seconds. If it takes longer, the UX needs work.

## Why This Works (Neurologically)

Decision fatigue is real. Every choice costs cognitive energy.

When you constrain the problem space:
- Fewer decisions → more energy for creative choices
- Clearer feedback loops
- Faster iteration

The best tool is the one you know deeply, not the one with the most features.

## Exceptions

Sometimes you *need* unlimited flexibility:
- Research exploration (don't know what you're looking for)
- Debugging (need visibility into everything)
- Mastering (final 1% of polish)

But for ideation and creation? **Constraints are features, not bugs.**
