Working on FlueBricks has taught me something counterintuitive: **more freedom in design often leads to worse instruments**.

When I first started letting musicians prototype their own flutes, I assumed they'd push boundaries—create wild, experimental shapes that challenged traditional acoustics. Instead, most gravitated toward familiar forms with minor tweaks.

## The Paradox of Choice

Give someone complete freedom in CAD software, and they freeze. The design space is too vast. But give them **modular constraints**—a library of pre-tested components that snap together—and suddenly creativity flourishes.

This mirrors what I've observed in music production tools. The best beat-makers aren't the ones with unlimited sample libraries. They're the ones who master a **small, well-chosen palette**.

## Digital Fabrication as a Thinking Tool

3D printing's real value isn't just speed—it's **iteration as conversation**. You print, test, hear the flaws, adjust. The instrument teaches you what it wants to be.

Traditional instrument makers learned this through apprenticeship. Now we can compress that feedback loop from years to days.

## What I'm Exploring Next

Can we encode acoustic heuristics directly into CAD tools? Instead of "make this tube 5mm wider," the interface could suggest: "this change will brighten the high register but destabilize the lowest note."

The goal isn't to replace expertise—it's to make tacit knowledge explicit and transferable.
