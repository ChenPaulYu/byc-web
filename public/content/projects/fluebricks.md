*FlueBricks turns flute-like instrument design into a hands-on reasoning process — learners build, listen, and revise modular pieces to discover how structure shapes sound.*

## What is FlueBricks?

FlueBricks is a modular toolkit that **decomposes flute-making into interchangeable, 3D-printed components** designed for acoustic reasoning. It consists of three component families:

- **Generators** produce the initial tone by **transforming breath into self-sustained oscillation** — decomposed into 8 submodules that map to critical acoustic geometries like flue tunnel, windway, and splitting edge.
- **Resonators** shape pitch and timbre by **adjusting the effective length of the resonating pipe** — including basic nodes, branch nodes, tuning nodes, and tweak rings.
- **Connectors** link modules, **route airflow across multiple chambers**, and hybridize with existing instruments.

![FlueBricks module overview. Standard modules (left) offer immediate playability for beginners; advanced modules (right) expose fine-grained acoustic parameters for expert exploration.](/content/projects/fluebricks/2-handbook.jpg)

## Designer–Player Loop

FlueBricks is built around the *designer–player loop*: users **iteratively switch between configuring instruments and playing them**, using each sonic outcome as evidence to refine their understanding. This tight interleaving of making and listening — **hypothesizing, configuring, playing, and refining** — is what drives *acoustic reasoning*.

![The designer–player loop in action. P12 probes resonator length limits, discovers multiphonics near the resonance threshold, then experiments with tone-hole placement — finding that position, not just size, governs pitch bending.](/content/projects/fluebricks/14-result-designer-player.jpg "https://youtu.be/PlZRS0iijbg")

## Acoustic Reasoning

![The acoustic reasoning cycle. Users hypothesize how structure might shape sound, configure modules to test, play to hear the result, and refine their understanding — then iterate.](/content/projects/fluebricks/acoustic-reasoning-cycle.svg)

In our study, participants didn't just "use" the system — they began to form and test hypotheses about how structure shapes sound. This emergent process manifested in four behaviors:

- **Instrument Scaffolding** — using familiar instruments (like a recorder) as a starting point for design
- **Rule Formation** — articulating "aha" moments that link geometry to acoustic behavior
- **Reinterpretation** — repurposing components in unexpected ways beyond their intended function
- **Performative Acts** — consolidating discoveries into playable instruments for music and teaching

![Exploration timelines of all 12 participants. Each row shows one session across three phases. Point markers denote reasoning events — scaffolding (blue stars), rule formation (green circles), and reinterpretation (purple diamonds). Horizontal bars indicate performative acts.](/content/projects/fluebricks/15-result-timeline.jpg)

### Instrument Scaffolding

Participants **drew on familiar instruments to ground their reasoning**. P1 framed goals as *"I want to make something like a recorder"*; P8 compared outcomes to a Dizi; P12 transferred overblowing techniques from his flute training. Across phases, scaffolding shifted from **descriptive** (*"this sounds like..."*) to **explanatory** (*"this works because..."*) to **creative intent**.

### Rule Formation

Participants **articulated generalizable acoustic principles**. P5 hypothesized, *"If I make the tube longer, maybe the sound will drop,"* then tested it. P6 observed that adjusting the splitting edge improved onset stability: *"Ah, now it speaks more easily."* Some built knowledge through **incremental micro-cycles**, while others formed rules only after **broader exploratory jumps**.

### Reinterpretation

Participants **repurposed modules beyond their intended acoustic roles**, revealing the toolkit's modular flexibility.

![User reinterpretations. (a) Tuning node reimagined as a standalone dizi-like generator. (b) Tweak ring used as a glissando controller. (c) Air hub configured for dual-player use. (d) Bridge connector as a free-floating pitch slider.](/content/projects/fluebricks/16-result-reinterpreted.jpg "https://youtu.be/Pe8k3IFLhOw")

## Performative Acts

Beyond reasoning cycles, participants consolidated their exploration into *performative acts* — using their creations as media for expression or teaching.

### Pedagogical Showing

P5 (Pro Woodwind) built a **structured teaching strategy**: systematically recording pitches, demonstrating the Westminster Quarters melody by swapping resonators, and proposing a classroom game — *"children should discover the pitches on their own."* P11 (Pro Musician) took an **open-ended approach**, suggesting learners imitate environmental sounds and articulating a philosophy of *"curiosity before music theory."*

![Pedagogical showing. Top: P5's progression from systematic pitch recording to melody demonstration to a classroom game proposal. Bottom: P11's open-ended approach — timbre exploration, sound mimicry activities, and discovery-first philosophy.](/content/projects/fluebricks/17-result-pedagogical-showing.jpg)

### Musical Expression

P12 (Pro Woodwind) configured a **custom multi-note setup** and performed *My Heart Will Go On*, transposing the melody when the original key exceeded the instrument's range. As he explained: *"Because we learn a lot of different instruments... I am familiar with finding a fingering by my own based on my own sense."*

![Musical expression. Left: P12 performs a transposed melody of My Heart Will Go On. Right: free improvisation across eight distinct pitches. Pitch contours extracted using SwiftF0.](/content/projects/fluebricks/18-result-musical-expression.jpg)

## Final Designs

In the final phase, participants were asked to **design a "classroom flute for elementary students"** — a concrete scenario requiring them to apply everything they had learned. Their strategies varied widely: some reused earlier prototypes, while others **built entirely new instruments drawing on acoustic principles** discovered during exploration. P7 submerged a resonator in water for *"strange and fun"* timbres; P8 reinterpreted the bridge connector as a tunable resonator; P6 and P10 emphasized imaginative shapes — a gun, a castle — to appeal to young learners. **11 out of 12 participants produced musically or pedagogically intentional instruments**, affirming FlueBricks's capacity to support creative, goal-driven instrument construction.

![Completed instruments from the scenario-based design task. All participants except P3, regardless of musical background, successfully built playable flutes using FlueBricks.](/content/projects/fluebricks/19-result-works.jpg "https://youtu.be/Gcw9wUSN-W8")
